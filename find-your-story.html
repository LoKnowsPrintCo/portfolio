Case study · Find Your Story

Find Your Story

A guided self-interview that helps people understand themselves before they hand their story to AI.

Six weeks Solo build PDF → web app Tested with friends Demo password: lodestar
01 The problem

I built this because I couldn't see my own ADHD.

I had an epiphany that I wanted to understand myself as someone with ADHD, and I still couldn't quite see it in my own life. Then it clicked. Now that AI exists, I could have an expert read my story back to me through their lens and tell me whether the pattern had been there the whole time. All I had to do was hand over the story.

The first time I did it, I cried. Then I walked a few friends through the same questions, and it happened to them too. That reaction is the reason this became a product instead of a private exercise. I love hearing people's stories, and hearing myself as the hero of one turned out to be the whole thing.

02 The hypothesis

Most people give AI fragments of themselves and get generic insight back. I believed that if someone was guided through their whole story first, in a structured way, and then handed that story to AI with a specific expert lens, the reading would be dramatically more meaningful.

Reflection first. Interpretation second.

03 How I made decisions

Four principles decided almost everything, including what I removed.

P1

Understanding before optimization

Orient the person first. Don't rush them toward an answer they haven't reached.

P2

Reflection is emotional work

So build in permission to pause. Long reflection is tiring, and people need somewhere to rest.

P3

It should feel like a journal, not software

Calm, not clinical. If it reads like a form, the reflection dies.

P4

The person is the author

They write their story; AI helps them see it. The app never writes it for them. This one turned out to be the spine.

04 The journey

This wasn't a long process. Six weeks ago it was a PDF, just a list of questions. Here is how it changed, and why.

Moved the flow to the left

The section navigation used to sit at the bottom. Moving it to a left-side flow made the whole thing feel more elevated, more like a journal you move through than a page you scroll.

Rebuilt the Prompt Builder

The handoff to AI read too technical, like a step for engineers. I made it button-like and interactive, so passing your story to a lens feels like part of the experience instead of a coding task.

Softened the language throughout

Small wording changes across the app, aimed at one feeling: this is a journal, not a piece of software.

Added Carl Jung, and rounded out the lenses

Two friends I showed it to both asked, "can you do Jung?" The lens set instantly felt incomplete without him. When it's obvious to the people using it, it's missing.

What it became is a workspace with four parts. It walks you through the questions, hands you the expert lenses to read your story through, helps you build the prompt that hunts for threads across those readings, and gives you one place to keep everything and come back to it. The AI reading itself happens in your own AI right now, through prompts the app builds for you. What the app owns is the writing, the organizing, and the returning.

05 What I chose not to build

The removals say more about this product than the features do. Everything I took out, I took out to protect one thing: the person stays the author. Of their words, and of their conclusions.

✕ AI writing your story for you

It would have been easy to let AI generate the reflections. But your own words are the product. The app helps you write your story and hand it to a lens. It never writes the story for you.

✕ A forced identity

I built this first for late-diagnosed ADHD women, then opened it up. Not to sell it to everyone. Some people won't understand themselves as ADHD or autistic, and I don't want the design to jar them into that conclusion before they're ready. So I turned the ADHD focus from an assumption into a choice. If someone wants that lens, it's right there for them to pick up.

✕ "After the Story"

I built a collection of a hundred literary characters you could continue your story through. I loved it, and I cut it. Paired with the ADHD framing, it made the experience prescriptive. It told people who they were before they had decided.

✕ Accounts, for now

Version 1 stays simple on purpose. The complexity of logins and sync wasn't worth it yet, and it would have slowed down the part that matters.

Author of the writing. Author of the meaning.

06 What I learned

The clearest thing wasn't in the interface. It was the reaction. Every person I walked through the questions cried, on every lens, including me, and not once. Every time. I stopped treating that as a fluke and started treating it as the point. Hearing yourself as the hero of your own story, read back through someone else's eyes, is the entire experience.

The smaller lesson humbled me. A friend suggested letting people bookmark a question to come back to later. My first reaction was that it was unnecessary, one more thing on every question. I was wrong. It became the small, functional touch that makes the app feel usable and cared-for. I don't always know which little thing will matter to someone using this differently than I do.

07 If I keep building
  • Read it aloud. The most transformative part is hearing your story in a voice that isn't yours, not just reading it. Browser speech synthesis is the feature I'm most impatient for.
  • Bring the AI inside. Right now the app builds prompts you run in your own AI. The next version weaves the interpretation in, so the app itself can say "I noticed this" as you go.
  • Easier cross-lens comparison. The threads feature works, but it's still copy and paste. I want the app to place readings side by side and surface what runs through all of them on its own.

Find your story when you're ready. Whichever story that is.
